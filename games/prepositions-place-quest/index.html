Prepositions Of Place: In, On, At

Prepositions of place go before nouns and tell us where something or someone is. The most useful beginner question is: are we thinking about a space, a surface, or a point/activity place?

Quick picture: in often means inside a space, on often means touching a surface or line, and at often means a point, address, event, or activity place. Some common expressions must be learned as chunks.

1. Use in for inside spaces

Use in when something is inside a bigger space or inside an area with limits.

  • in the bag, in the kitchen, in the classroom
  • in Cairo, in France, in the mountains
  • in a car or in a taxi

2. Use in for water and printed material

We also use in when something is inside water, or appears inside a book, picture, newspaper, or document.

  • swim in the sea, in the river, in the pool
  • the woman in the picture
  • information in the newspaper

3. Use on for surfaces

Use on when something is touching a surface.

  • on the table, on the desk, on the sofa
  • on the wall, on the floor
  • on the third floor

4. Use on for transport, streets, lines, and media

Use on for public transport and for many lines or surfaces we imagine as lines.

  • on the bus, on a train, on a plane
  • on James Street, on the border, on the River Thames
  • on TV, on the internet, on a website

5. Use at for points and exact positions

Use at when the place is understood as a point, meeting place, address, entrance, or exact position.

  • at the door, at the entrance, at the back of the room
  • at 15 Craig Street
  • at a desk can mean sitting/working at that position.

6. Use at for activity places

Use at in common phrases where the place suggests an activity.

  • at school, at work, at home
  • at the library, at the supermarket, at the station
  • at a party, at a concert

7. Compare common contrasts

Some contrasts are about how we imagine the place.

  • in a car, but on a bus/train/plane
  • in a lake means in the water; on a lake can mean on the surface or beside it.
  • in the park can mean inside the park area; at the park can mean the park as a meeting place.

8. Learn useful chunks

Some phrases are so common that it is best to learn them as complete chunks.

  • at home, at work, at school
  • on the left, on the right
  • in the world, in the countryside, in the forest
Final teacher tip: do not translate prepositions word by word. First ask what kind of place meaning you need: inside space, surface/line, or point/activity place. Then check whether the sentence is one of the common chunks.
